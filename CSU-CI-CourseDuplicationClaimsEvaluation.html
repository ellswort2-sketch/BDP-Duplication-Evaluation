Evaluation of CSU Channel Islands Course Duplication Claims

Course-level review of selected CSU Channel Islands courses and proposed Coalinga College upper-division courses in the Agriculture Science and Technology B.S.

PSLO Analysis | Course Duplication Review | Core Requirements Review

Basis for Review

This review evaluates course duplication, not mere topical relatedness. The controlling question is whether the paired courses are substantially equivalent in instructional purpose, course level, content scope, expected competencies, and role within the degree. Shared vocabulary or shared disciplinary setting is not enough to establish duplication.

The CSU Channel Islands courses considered here are associated with sustainable agriculture, biology, environmental science, and agricultural practice. The Coalinga College courses are part of a proposed Agriculture Science and Technology B.S. built around precision agriculture, sensing systems, spatial analytics, automation, AI/ML, IoT, autonomous equipment, variable-rate application, and technology adoption in agricultural operations.

Course Pair Analyses

AGST 325 and ESRM 328

Coalinga College: AGST 325 Spatial Data Analysis and Decision Support Systems

AGST 325 applies geospatial analysis to agricultural decision-making. The course emphasizes geostatistical analysis, multi-sensor agricultural data integration, soil data, yield maps, weather data, remote sensing, machine-learning models, field variability mapping, and economic evaluation of precision agriculture strategies.

CSU Channel Islands: ESRM 328 The Why of Where: Foundations in GIS

ESRM 328 introduces principles and applications of Geographic Information Systems for environmental analysis and resource management. The course is centered on foundational GIS concepts and environmental/resource-management applications.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. The common element is GIS, but the instructional purposes are different. ESRM 328 provides GIS foundations for environmental and resource-management contexts. AGST 325 assumes prior GIS and precision agriculture preparation and extends into agricultural geostatistics, multi-source field data integration, machine-learning decision support, and ROI analysis. This is related subject matter, not course duplication.

AGST 345 and BIOL 495

Coalinga College: AGST 345 Industry Certification and Internship

AGST 345 is an integrated capstone for the agricultural technology sector. Students complete supervised work experience while preparing for industry-relevant certifications such as PCA, CCA, and/or FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot. The course also includes professional readiness, networking, portfolio development, technical problem-solving in a professional setting, and reflection on career pathways.

CSU Channel Islands: BIOL 495 Agriculture Internship

BIOL 495 provides supervised work and study for students interested in becoming Pest Control Advisers. Students learn research and technical skills related to plant health, integrated pest management, or production systems. The course may involve service learning and includes a weekly seminar on current agricultural industry issues. It is repeatable by topic up to 9 units.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. Both are supervised experiential courses, but supervised experience is a course format rather than a complete instructional equivalency. BIOL 495 is oriented toward agricultural internship experience, PCA preparation, plant health, IPM, and production systems. AGST 345 is an agricultural technology capstone that combines internship experience with certification planning, portfolio development, and application of technology-oriented competencies. The duplicated element is the internship structure, not the course content or program function.

AGST 360 and BIOL 471 / SUAG 410

Coalinga College: AGST 360 Soil Fertility & Precision Nutrient Management

AGST 360 integrates soil fertility with precision application technologies. The course addresses nutrient formulations, crop-specific nutrient uptake, macro- and micronutrients, nutrient mobility, deficiency symptoms, remote and real-time crop and soil sensing, AI/ML nutrient-demand forecasting, irrigation system optimization, pulsing, spoon-feeding, controlled-release fertilizer, and variable-rate nutrient applications.

CSU Channel Islands: BIOL 471 Soil Science and SUAG 410 Plant Nutrition

BIOL 471 provides fundamental understanding of soil systems, including soil organisms, microbe-plant interactions, nutrient cycling, soil fertility, plant production, soil as habitat, environmental quality, and global environmental change. SUAG 410 provides an in-depth exploration of plant nutrition, essential nutrients, nutrient uptake mechanisms, physiological processes supporting plant growth, soil-plant interactions, nutrient cycling, nutrient availability, sustainable fertilization strategies, integrated soil health management, precision agriculture approaches, and theoretical models used to optimize plant nutrition in cropping systems.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. BIOL 471 and SUAG 410 provide soil science and plant nutrition foundations, including fertility, nutrient cycling, soil-plant relations, and sustainable fertilization. AGST 360 is narrower and more implementation-focused: it requires students to use precision agriculture technologies to make spatially and temporally targeted nutrient management decisions. Its defining content is the integration of nutrient management with sensing, AI/ML forecasting, irrigation-system timing, controlled-release strategies, spoon-feeding, and variable-rate application. Shared soil-fertility content does not make the courses duplicative.

AGST 365 and BIOL 472

Coalinga College: AGST 365 Advanced Technologies for Integrated Pest Management

AGST 365 integrates advanced agricultural technologies with IPM principles. Students apply ecological and economic thresholds with remote sensing, automated monitoring, autonomous systems, UAS, machine vision, IoT-based pest detection, and machine-learning pest-pressure and crop-growth-stage models. The course requires evaluation of technology-enabled IPM plans using pest-control, environmental-risk, and economic metrics.

CSU Channel Islands: BIOL 472 Integrated Pest Management

BIOL 472 presents integrated pest management as a comprehensive approach to monitoring and controlling agricultural pests in an environmentally acceptable manner. The course addresses ecological principles of pest management as they relate to plant pathogens, weeds, and arthropod pests. It also covers natural predators, cultural practices, chemical applications, and current pest management practices in local agricultural systems.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. BIOL 472 provides a conventional IPM treatment centered on ecological principles and management strategies. AGST 365 uses IPM principles as a foundation for advanced technology-enabled pest management. Its distinguishing competencies are digital monitoring, UAS-based observation, machine vision, IoT pest detection, machine-learning prediction, autonomous systems, and quantitative evaluation of IPM performance. The presence of IPM in both courses establishes disciplinary relatedness, not duplication.

AGST 370 and SUAG 400

Coalinga College: AGST 370 Agriculture 4.0 Weed Management

AGST 370 integrates biological, mechanical/physical, cultural, and chemical weed-management principles with machine vision-based weed detection, AI/ML decision systems, laser weeding, autonomous spraying, remote sensing workflows, variable-rate herbicide application, economic analysis, environmental analysis, and regulatory considerations for technology-enabled weed management systems.

CSU Channel Islands: SUAG 400 Weed Management

SUAG 400 provides advanced exploration of weed management principles, including weed ecology, identification, and control strategies in agricultural and natural ecosystems. Students examine integrated weed management, herbicide modes of action, biological and cultural controls, the economic and environmental impact of weeds, sustainable practices, regulatory considerations, and new technological advancements in weed control. Students develop management plans aligned with ecological sustainability and agricultural productivity.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. SUAG 400 is the closest topical match because both courses address weed management. Even so, AGST 370 is not a general weed-management course. Its central purpose is Agriculture 4.0 implementation: machine vision, AI/ML, laser weeding, autonomous spraying, remote sensing workflows, and variable-rate application platforms. SUAG 400 provides broad weed-management coverage across agricultural and natural ecosystems. The courses are related within weed science, but the Coalinga course has a distinct technology-integration and implementation role.

AGST 380 and BIOL 452

Coalinga College: AGST 380 Technology-Enabled Agricultural Entomology

AGST 380 examines insects and mites of economic importance to Central Valley agriculture. It integrates pest identification, life cycles, host relationships, ecological interactions, AI/ML models, growing degree days, weather variables, historical pest data, spatial and automated pest monitoring, robotic and autonomous platforms, machine vision, remote sensing, and targeted or variable-rate control measures.

CSU Channel Islands: BIOL 452 Entomology

BIOL 452 explores insect biology, including morphology, physiology, ecology, behavior, taxonomy, and evolutionary history of major insect orders. The course addresses the importance of insects to humans and biological control. Laboratories emphasize collection, mounting, preservation, and identification of insect specimens to order and family.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. BIOL 452 is a broad biological entomology course with laboratory specimen work and insect identification. AGST 380 is an agricultural pest-management course using entomological knowledge to support digital forecasting, spatial pest mapping, remote sensing, autonomous application systems, and variable-rate pest management. The courses share insect-related content but prepare students for different competencies.

AGST 385 and SUAG 300

Coalinga College: AGST 385 On-Farm Research and Decision-Making

AGST 385 trains students to design, implement, analyze, and communicate on-farm research trials. The course emphasizes experimental layouts, controls, replication, hypothesis testing, statistical tools, monitoring technologies, data management, remote sensing, IoT sensors, drones, automated environmental monitoring, agronomic significance, economic returns, risk, and ROI.

CSU Channel Islands: SUAG 300 Farming Practices

SUAG 300 is an experiential course in principles and practices of farming. Topics include soil fertility, pest management, planting, harvesting, and irrigation. Students apply concepts and skills in organic vegetables, berries, and/or fruit tree orchards depending on season and farm needs. Harvested food is donated to the campus community or sold at a farm stand or farmers market.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. SUAG 300 teaches practical farming skills in a production setting. AGST 385 teaches research design and evidence-based decision-making for commercial agricultural systems. Its central competencies are field-trial design, statistical defensibility, sensor-enabled data collection, interpretation of agronomic and economic significance, and development of research-based recommendations. Practical agricultural context appears in both courses, but the instructional purposes are different.

AGST 390 and SUAG 470

Coalinga College: AGST 390 Technology-driven Regenerative Agriculture

AGST 390 examines regenerative agriculture as an adaptive approach to soil health, biodiversity, ecosystem resilience, productivity, and profitability. The course emphasizes AI/ML models, remote sensing, IoT networks, drones, precision management systems, autonomous equipment, COMET-Farm, quantitative monitoring, ecological and economic evaluation, and adaptive management based on technology-generated data.

CSU Channel Islands: SUAG 470 Regenerative Agriculture

SUAG 470 examines the promises and pitfalls of regenerative agriculture as a climate solution. The course discusses approaches to regenerative agriculture principles, practices, and outcomes by spotlighting farmers and initiatives that aim to increase climate resiliency through soil health and biodiversity. Students learn about regenerative agriculture certifications, government programs, and contemporary debates.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. Both courses address regenerative agriculture, but they do so for different instructional ends. SUAG 470 emphasizes regenerative agriculture as a climate and sustainability topic, including principles, practices, farmer initiatives, certifications, government programs, and debates. AGST 390 is a technology-driven implementation course requiring students to use AI/ML, remote sensing, IoT, drones, precision systems, autonomous equipment, and COMET-Farm to monitor, model, and evaluate regenerative outcomes. The shared regenerative agriculture theme does not establish course duplication.

AGST 395 and SUAG 370 / SUAG 410

Coalinga College: AGST 395 Smart Plant Health Management Systems

AGST 395 examines plant disease biology, disease cycles, host-pathogen interactions, remote sensing, UAS-based imaging, IoT-enabled spore and disease monitoring, AI/ML-powered detection, predictive disease models, spatial disease assessment, targeted interventions, autonomous systems, and variable-rate applications within integrated plant health management systems.

CSU Channel Islands: SUAG 370 Smart Agriculture and SUAG 410 Plant Nutrition

SUAG 370 explores the role of modern technologies in agriculture to promote sustainability, efficiency, and food security. Students gain a high-level understanding of precision agriculture, remote sensing, IoT, AI, robotics, data analytics, and integration into real-world agricultural systems, with attention to ethical, environmental, and social impacts. SUAG 410 focuses on plant nutrition, essential nutrients, nutrient uptake, physiological processes, soil-plant interactions, nutrient cycling, nutrient availability, sustainable fertilization, integrated soil health management, precision agriculture approaches, and theoretical models for optimizing plant nutrition.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. SUAG 370 is a broad smart-agriculture survey accessible to students without a technical background, while SUAG 410 is a plant nutrition course. AGST 395 is specifically a smart plant-health and plant disease management course. Its defining content is pathogen biology, disease cycles, disease diagnostics, disease-risk prediction, spore monitoring, remote and UAS imaging for disease detection, AI/ML detection, spatial disease assessment, and targeted intervention. Neither SUAG 370 nor SUAG 410 duplicates that plant-disease-centered systems focus.

ECON 360 and SUAG 370

Coalinga College: ECON 360 Precision Agriculture Economics and Technology Adoption

ECON 360 integrates economic analysis, market evaluation, and strategic planning for precision agriculture technology adoption in California's Central Valley. Students conduct cost-benefit analyses, assess ROI, evaluate scalability for technologies such as automated machinery and IoT-based sensing systems, evaluate market, policy, and environmental factors affecting adoption, and develop economically justified technology adoption plans.

CSU Channel Islands: SUAG 370 Smart Agriculture

SUAG 370 explores the transformative role of modern technologies in agriculture to promote sustainability, efficiency, and food security. Students gain a high-level understanding of precision agriculture, remote sensing, IoT, AI, robotics, and data analytics and how these tools are integrated into real-world agricultural systems. The course also emphasizes ethical, environmental, and social impacts of technological change in agriculture and remains accessible to students without a technical background.

Duplication finding: The courses are not substantially equivalent. SUAG 370 surveys modern agricultural technologies and their broader sustainability, efficiency, food-security, ethical, environmental, and social implications. ECON 360 is an economics and adoption-planning course. Its central competencies are cost-benefit analysis, ROI assessment, scalability evaluation, market and policy analysis, risk consideration, and preparation of economically justified adoption plans. The fact that both courses mention agricultural technology does not duplicate the economics content and adoption-analysis function of ECON 360.

Conclusion

The course pairings reviewed here show repeated areas of disciplinary relatedness, especially in sustainable agriculture, weed management, IPM, soil fertility, plant nutrition, entomology, regenerative agriculture, internships, and smart agriculture. Those areas of relatedness do not rise to substantial course duplication.

The Coalinga College courses are consistently structured around technology-integrated agricultural implementation: geostatistics, sensors, remote sensing, AI/ML, IoT, autonomous systems, variable-rate application, predictive models, decision-support systems, and economic adoption analysis. The CSU Channel Islands courses generally emphasize sustainable agriculture, biology, environmental science, ecological principles, plant and soil systems, general smart-agriculture awareness, and applied farming experience.

On the descriptions reviewed, the course comparisons support a finding of no substantial duplication. The courses are related within the broad field of agriculture, but they do not have the same instructional purpose, technical depth, expected competencies, or role within the degree programs.
