CSUF Core Course Duplication Claims

Course Duplication Claims: Coalinga College and CSU Fresno

Review of claimed course equivalencies and overlaps.

Purpose. This page evaluates the course-level duplication claims identified in the CSU Fresno matrix. Course titles can be selected to show or hide descriptions. The analysis focuses on the courses that remain after excluding transfer, articulated, and GE coursework.

Estimated Actual Overlap After Applying the Four-Elective Limit

The CSUF Agricultural Systems Management degree allows only four major electives, or 12 units. Pages 10-12 of the duplication analysis provided by CSUF (Fresno-Duplication Analysis-Coalinga.pdf) provides a list of CSUF courses that CC BDP courses duplicate. In this list, CSUF has only 5 core courses, the remaining 15 CSUF courses are electives. That is a significant and important weakness in the CSUF duplication analysis. A CSUF student would complete only 4 of the elective courses used in the matrix that are claimed to overlap with the Coalinga College BDP.

A reasonable estimate of actual overlap is about 20 to 30 percent. The upper end assumes a student selects the four CSUF electives with the strongest relationship to the Coalinga College curriculum. The lower end reflects the fact that several comparisons are only partial, survey-level, or based on general industrial technology rather than agriculture-specific systems integration.

Interpretive Standard

The key question is not whether two courses use similar terminology. The issue is whether they are similar in level, required status, instructional depth, laboratory or field application, agricultural context, and degree function.

1. AET 005 compared with IT 52

includes required CSUF course

Claim type: CSUF claims equivalent curriculum and curriculum overlap.

Coalinga College Course or Courses

AET 005Fundamentals of Electronics (DC)CC lower-division major preparation
Introduces electricity and electronics, electrical components, tools, measurement devices, and DC circuits. This analysis treats the course as 36 lecture hours and 54 laboratory hours, all focused on applied DC electronics.

CSU Fresno Course or Courses

IT 52Electricity and ElectronicsCSUF required course
Introduction to electrostatics, AC and DC circuits, electrical calculations, magnetics, circuit applications, electrical measuring and test equipment, schematics, wiring diagrams, standards, and codes. This analysis treats the course as 54 total instructional hours.

Evaluation: Partial overlap, but not equivalent.

AET 005 is entirely focused on applied DC electronics and includes 36 lecture hours plus 54 lab hours. IT 52 covers both AC and DC electronics in 54 total instructional hours. If split evenly, that implies approximately 27 hours of DC instruction. The courses share a focus on DC electronics but differ in extent of instruction on that topic. Based on instructional hours, there is a 43% overlap between these two course.

3. CRPSCI 006, CRPSCI 008, CRPSCI 018, and AGST 325 compared with IT 186

elective-dependent claim | includes required CSUF course

Claim type: CSUF uses IT 186 to support multiple duplication claims.

Coalinga College Course or Courses

CRPSCI 006Introduction to Precision AgricultureCC lower-division major preparation
Introduction to precision agriculture, including the technological tools and processes used as an introductory foundation.
CRPSCI 008Applications of Geospatial TechnologyCC lower-division major preparation
Surveys geospatial technologies in agriculture and related fields, with a focus on GPS, GIS, and remote sensing.
CRPSCI 018Precision Ag SoftwareCC lower-division major preparation

AGST 325Spatial Data Analysis and Decision Support SystemsCC upper-division major course
Geospatial analysis for decision making, with emphasis on geostatistical analysis, multi-sensor data integration, and machine learning to generate field-scale management insights.

CSU Fresno Course or Courses

IT 186Applied Spatial TechnologyCSUF required or elective in matrix context
Survey of GIS/GPS, remote sensing, imaging technology, geodatabases, logistics, agriculture, business, precision agriculture, food systems, and public policy. This analysis treats the course as a 54-hour lecture survey course.

Evaluation: Substantial overstatement. One survey course cannot substitute for a multi-course applied sequence.

IT 186 is a 54-hour lecture survey course. The four CC courses collectively provide about 162 lecture hours and 162 lab/application hours, or 324 total instructional hours. Thus, assuming all the material covered in IT 186 is also covered in the CC course sequence, IT 186 would represents 17% of the instructional hours of the CC course sequence. The CC sequence includes field data, interpolation, prescription maps, FMIS, geostatistics, spatial uncertainty, SGeMS, and decision support systems.

Potential curriculum revision to reduce perceived duplication: One option would be for Coalinga College to create a transfer-level GIS/GPS course as part of the Geography AS degree and as a requirement for the Agriculture Science and Technology AS degree. CRPSCI 008 would then be inactivated, and CRPSCI 006 and CRPSCI 018 would be revised to minimize GPS/GIS content. That would completely eliminate any duplication.

Under this revision, CRPSCI 018 would shift away from general GIS/GPS instruction and instead focus on an introduction to geostatistics, farm management information systems, and carbon farm planning tools such as COMET-Farm. This would further distinguish the Coalinga College sequence from IT 186.

The proposed geography transfer-level GIS course could align with C-ID GEOG 155, Introduction to Geographic Information Systems and Techniques, with Lab. The C-ID GEOG 155 description is: “Study of Geographic Information Systems (GIS) science and its applications to spatial data management. Identification and acquisition of GIS data. Assessment of vector and raster systems, scale, resolution, map projection, coordinate systems, georeferencing and Global Positioning Systems (GPS). Spatial analysis and modeling with GIS.”

Overall Evaluation

After excluding transfer, articulated, and GE courses, the two degrees show some contact in broad areas such as geospatial tools, automation, robotics, project management, and agricultural systems terminology. However, the actual overlap is constrained by the four-elective limit and by the imperfect alignment between the compared courses.

The most favorable four-elective selection could improve alignment in a few areas, such as industrial process control, big data, project management, or robotics. Even then, much of the Coalinga College BDP remains distinct because of its emphasis on agricultural technology integration, IoT sensing, UAV and remote sensing workflows, geostatistics, spatial uncertainty, AI/ML decision support, CAN Bus and ISOBUS integration, SCADA in agricultural systems, and field-scale implementation.

For that reason, the actual overlap is best estimated at approximately 20 to 30 percent, not as a majority of the degree. The programs share some vocabulary and enabling technologies, but the curriculum structure and applied emphasis remain substantially different.
