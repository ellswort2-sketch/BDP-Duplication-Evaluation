Course Duplication Claims: Coalinga College and CSU Fresno

Interactive evaluation of claimed course equivalencies and overlaps with expandable course descriptions.

Purpose: This document evaluates course-level duplication claims identified in the CSU Fresno course matrix. Select any course title to expand or collapse the course description. The analysis distinguishes required courses, electives, excluded transfer/GE courses, instructional hours, and the structural limit that CSUF Ag Sys Mgt students may complete only four major electives.

Key Structural Issue: Four-Elective Limit

The CSUF Ag Systems Management degree allows only four major electives, or 12 units. This matters because the course matrix relies heavily on CSUF electives to support overlap claims. Many claimed overlaps cannot all be true in a single student pathway because the electives are mutually exclusive once the four-elective limit is reached.

The 50.8% figure should be treated as a theoretical upper bound, not a realistic degree-overlap estimate. It assumes strategic elective selection and does not account for imperfect alignment, prerequisite consumption, differences in instructional hours, survey versus advanced applied depth, or industrial-technology versus agriculture-specific context.

A more defensible realistic overlap estimate is approximately 20%–35%, because many claimed overlaps are partial, elective-dependent, or based on broad topical similarity rather than equivalent curriculum.

Interpretive Standard

The most important question is not whether two courses share keywords, but whether they are equivalent in instructional depth, level, context, required/elective status, applied field/lab experience, and degree function.

Overall Evaluation of Percent Overlap

The uploaded matrix reports 33 duplicated major units out of 65 total major units, or 50.8%. That percentage is best understood as a theoretical maximum created by counting required courses plus a strategically selected set of electives. It is not a reliable estimate of actual degree duplication.

The four-elective limit means that many elective-based claims cannot be realized simultaneously. Some claimed equivalencies require two CSUF electives to approximate one CC course, and PLANT 167 also requires PLANT 161 as a prerequisite, consuming additional elective capacity. Therefore, even under generous assumptions, elective-based overlap is capped and internally constrained.

After accounting for the elective cap, imperfect course alignment, survey-versus-applied sequence differences, agriculture-specific integration, laboratory/application hours, and the central role of AI/ML, IoT, geostatistics, remote sensing, and precision decision support in the CC BDP, a reasonable realistic overlap estimate is approximately 20%–35%.

The two degrees therefore show some topical contact in geospatial tools, automation, project management, and agricultural systems language, but they are not substantially duplicative in curriculum structure, applied context, or degree emphasis.
